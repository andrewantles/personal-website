I wrote a single player mini-TTRPG, but didn't have a big player base, nor a lot of time to repeatedly playtest. Initial results were promising and provided the right "grind-y" feeling and the correct "pressure" on the player to act and adapt. I needed more though. I decided to launch a project to adapt my game into one that can be played in the browser. I also dreamt up a simulation component where I could run batches of games and analyze the turns and outcomes to achieve greater balance and potential enjoyment of the game.

Original inspiration was received when reading the second half of the James S. A. Corey *The Expanse* series. I don't want to give too much away if you haven't read it, because it's a fantastic series that builds on itself beautifully, but at some point an authoritarian-type regime is in power over the communities that our heroes find themselves in. There's much development of a resistance and the drama and intrigue that comes along with that. I thought to myself, *Man, I'd like to find a game or something where I could experience some of this risk-taking and high-stakes decision making in a tricky context like this!* Alas, I couldn't find anything that matches the feeling of what I was hoping for, neither in digital nor table-type games. So, I decided to make one myself! Enter *The Good Fight Mini-TTRPG (Solo)*.

The game itself is just a couple pages of instructions. I've always liked that about the Mini-TTRPG genre. I love the idea of the tactile simulation of a game world, but seriously, some of the core rulebooks are hundreds of pages long, and that's before expansions and mechanics add-ons. Sheesh. I wish I had that kind of spare leisure time. In my game, you find yourself fed up and ready to act, yet under the thumb of an authoritarian regime. You are a single visionary and begin by performing small acts (the only kind you can afford initially) to raise your influence and build a recruitment base. At the same time, however, heat is also rising as the regime increases its suspicion of you and starts to investigate your actions. You build your resistance and take on larger and larger acts until you are in a position to cause serious damage to the regime and its leverage and power sources. All while navigating and responding to heightened investigations and coordinated takedown actions. 

So, how did this work? Let's talk about what worked and what didn't work. I started, as I suspect a lot of us do, with an LLM "Plan Mode" session. I wrote up a lengthy prompt and tried to anticipate every detail I thought an LLM would need to execute the project. It developed multiple phases, and we began working through those. I exhaustively code reviewed every change, and began a `questions.md` document where I listed every concern I had with the code it was writing, *prior* to merging any PR. Additionally, I looked up every library and convention that I didn't already have experience with and learned about it. It. Took. Forever. I eventually ran out of time and the project stalled for several months, with several open PRs. 

What did work, then? I ended up watching a great talk by Matt Pocock, presented on the AI Engineering YouTube channel, [Full Walkthrough: Workflow for AI Coding](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-QFHIoCo-Ko), and a lot of what he spoke about resonated with me. "Who feels like they're working harder than ever with AI?" Most hands go up... He describes this horizontal phasing that AI likes to operate in. Develop the entire backend. Develop the entire control plane and business logic. Finish with the front end. That's exactly what my LLM tried to do! 

One of his arguments throughout is that programming fundamentals, written about in industry staples over the last 20 years, are more important today than ever. (Here's a more concise version of his talk that really emphasizes this part: [link](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v4F1gFy-hqg)). Matt proposes throwing out Plan Mode, and instead working to build a shared understanding of the project requirements, and codifying that upfront with the LLM. This is how an engineering team would approach a problem. He suggests skills and doc formats for maintaining "domain knowledge" related to the project, and storing these in version control along with the code, so they persist across LLM sessions. To simplify, after this back-and-forth "grilling" session, a project requirements document is created (`prd.md`, created using the `/to-prd` skill and stored in the root of the repo). Then, the PRD is used to create a series of GitHub issues with blocking relationships, using the `/to-tickets` skill. 

You wind up with a bunch of "vertical slices" of work that can be done unsupervised by the LLM. Matt argues that you should invest significant time in laying a proper project foundation, so you can really step away, stress-free, from the automated code writing portion. 

Matt's `/to-tickets` skill references a `/tdd` skill that I found really helpful. My initial attempt, in contrast, saw an agent generally writing tests after the code, unless I stopped it or asked upfront. It would then write an entire library of tests, and they were okay. With the "vertical slices" approach, you wind up with end-to-end functionality written at every turn, that is much simpler to write a failing test for upfront, and can immediately be tested by both human and machine - as opposed to a bunch of non-function application layers that don't run yet. There's more to this that I won't go into extreme detail about here, including Matt's integration-style test-writing guidance, and simple interface/deep module software architecture style. Let me just say that due to the easily parsable blocking relationships and bite-sized nature of the GitHub issues, parallelism is easily achieved. It's easy for an orchestrator LLM process to split up a bunch of git trees and subagents, and things move really quite fast once you reach this stage.

I will say, I stopped auditing every line of code. I'm a dad, and it's just not realistic. This is essentially a static webpage that runs JavaScript in a browser sandbox. There's not a lot to exploit here, and the risk profile is really low. Everything ended up being done in vanilla JavaScript, so no supply chain risk. (I will say, the UI is pretty bad. It's that weird navy/gold Claude default UI. I did not choose to spend cycles on the UI for this project. I chose to just let it be...). The only libraries needed are for the test harnesses, and this thing is running in an Anthropic-hosted sandbox 90% of the time, so again, virtually no risk. (Yes, I sit and watch every command when I need to work on my local machine. I built an agentic sandbox harness at work, but haven't taken the time yet to deploy it locally.) Suffice it to say. We sprinted to the end on this one and avoided the code review bottleneck that is required for real, production-level projects and apps.

To get a look at the latest version of Matt Pocock's skills, he maintains them on his GitHub: https://github.com/mattpocock/skills. He also has a series of free and paid courses that offer, linked from his GitHub as well. I'll be continuing to work with his approach on additional personal projects, and have already had boosted success at work implementing these principles!
